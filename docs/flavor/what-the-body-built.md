# What the Body Built

*A dream from the slice-10 design pass — the day the death boundary became
a principle (#36). Companion to [the-ledger-of-firsts.md](the-ledger-of-firsts.md);
same bridge, the other side of the table.*

---

Before the clerk and the reading, there is the porter.

The porter keeps the long shed at the near end of the bridge, and the soul
must pass through it, and the porter's job is the emptying. He is not
unkind. He has simply done this a very long time, and he knows what crosses
and what does not, the way a ferryman knows the weight of a coin without
looking at it.

"Set it down," the porter said.

"Set what down?"

"All of it. You'll feel lighter when you see what's left."

So the soul set it down, item by item, because there is no arguing in the
shed. The marrow first — forty years of tempering, laid on the counter like
a bundle of dark iron rods, still warm. Then the meridians, all twenty,
coiled and gleaming, and the soul flinched to see how beautiful they were
outside the body that had opened them one aching span at a time. The banked
qi went into a drain in the floor and made no sound at all, which was
somehow the worst of it. The sect badge. The cauldron-calluses. The
strength — the particular strength it had built facing the thing with its
own face, the hungry shadow, seasons of standing firm compressed into a
muscle the soul had thought was itself — the porter took that too, and
folded it, and the soul cried out.

"That one I *earned*."

"The body earned it," the porter said, not without gentleness. "The body
paid in fear and sat up nights and grew that strength out of its own
marrow, the way it grew the marrow. It's the body's. It stays with the
body's things." He set it on the shelf with the iron rods. "You keep having
this argument, you know. Most of you do. You point at the strength and say
*that was me*. It was never you. It was your house being strong."

The soul stood in the emptiness where its power had been, and it was about
to ask *then what is mine* when it noticed it was still holding things — 
had been holding them all along, in hands it no longer had.

That it had faced the shadow. Not the strength from the facing — the
*facing*. The fact of it, whole and weightless, like a scar that doesn't
mar. The porter did not reach for that one.

The way it had learned to sit unattended — the stillness a younger life had
bled a fortune of qi to learn, discipline by discipline, so the work would
go on while it slept. The porter's eyes passed over it as if it were the
soul's own shape.

Every glimpse. Every one — the sword's edge, the river's patience, the pair
of doors called Life and Death it had once stood between at dusk — dim now,
folded small, but *there*, the way you cannot unsee the shape in a puzzle
picture once it has resolved.

And the spring. The small cold spring in the eastern hills where a boy who
did not know he would become all of this had knelt and drunk and thought,
for no reason, *I should remember this.* It weighed nothing. It had always
weighed nothing. It was the heaviest thing the soul carried, and the porter
tipped his hat to it as it passed.

"Go on," the porter said. "The clerk is waiting, and you have a good page
this time." He was already turning to the shelves, to the iron and the
gold, the coiled light, the folded strength — the beautiful heavy things
souls leave in the shed, that were never anything but scaffolding.

"What happens to it all?" the soul asked from the door.

"The world takes it back," said the porter. "It always needed it more than
you did. Where do you think marrow comes from?"
