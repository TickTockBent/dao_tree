# The Ledger of Firsts

*A dream from the slice-10 design pass — the night the karma economy took its
shape. Companion to [the-third-corpse.md](the-third-corpse.md).*

---

At the far end of the bridge between lives there is a table, and at the table
sits a clerk, and before the clerk lies a book so large that its far edge is a
rumor. The soul arrived the way souls do — all at once, and without its name.

"Sit," said the clerk, not looking up. "This is the reading."

The book opened itself to the soul's page, and the clerk began to read the
life aloud. It was a good life. The soul had climbed the mountain called
Foundation in three years and the mountain called Core in nine, and it said
so, with some heat, when the clerk's brush did not move.

"Nine years," the soul said. "No life of mine has forged a core in nine
years. The elders called it a generational talent."

"Heaven watched you forge a core," the clerk agreed. "Four lives ago. It was
raining. You wept, and the rain and the weeping were indistinguishable, and
all the bureaus of the sky leaned out of their windows to see it done for the
first time." The clerk turned a page. "Heaven does not lean out of the window
for the fourth performance of a play, however crisp the acting."

"Then what was it all for?"

"Keep still. I am not finished reading."

The brush hung over the page like a hawk. The clerk read on: the sect joined
— *again*, the brush unmoved — the pills brewed — *again* — the stipend
drawn, the meridians opened, the same twelve gates in the same order as
always, and the soul grew smaller in its seat with every *again*, until the
clerk arrived at a small entry near the end, written in the cramped hand of
a life that had not thought anyone was watching.

"Here," said the clerk. "In your fifty-first year. A crowned thing came out
of your own heart and sat across from you wearing your face, and told you,
correctly, that you had never once been loved for anything but your talent.
And you did not break. You sat with it for a season and you did not break."

"The Hollow Crown," the soul said. "That was the worst year of the life."

"It was the only year of the life," said the clerk, and the brush came down,
and the sound it made on the page was the sound of a very large bell heard
from very far inside the earth. "You had never done that before. Not in any
body. Heaven has been waiting eleven lives to see whether you could."

The soul watched the ink dry. It was not a large payment. It was, the soul
understood suddenly, the only kind there was.

"The mountains, then," the soul said slowly, "are worthless."

"The mountains are *priced*," the clerk corrected. "Every mountain is worth
its first climbing. That is not a small thing — there are more mountains
than you have had lives, and the far ranges have never been walked by
anyone. But you keep circling the foothills where you already know the
paths." The clerk closed the book, and the wind of its closing smelled of
every rain the soul had ever forged anything in. "Heaven is not a
millstone, little cousin. It does not pay for turning. It is a reader, and
it pays for the page it has not read."

"And what do the heavens *do* with all of it?" the soul asked, at the lip of
the bridge, where the fog of the next womb was already rising. "All those
firsts, all those pages?"

The clerk looked up for the first time. Behind the table, behind the clerk,
the book's far edge was still a rumor, and the soul saw now that the rumor
went on in both directions, and that every page was a life, and not all of
the lives were its own.

"They are writing a book," the clerk said. "Try to be worth reading."
