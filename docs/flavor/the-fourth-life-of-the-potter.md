# The Fourth Life of the Potter

*A dream from the night transcendence shipped (slice-10 step 6, D39).
Three lives severing the same attachment; the fourth life begins already
past it — cap × contribution from breath one, no trough. This is what
that math feels like from the inside. Candidate journal material for a
transcended profession.*

---

In her first life she was a potter before she was a cultivator, and when
the Second Corpse demanded a true thing, she gave it the wheel. Six
turnings she was less than she had been. Her hands, emptied of clay,
would not stop making the shape of vessels in the air. She recovered.
She surpassed. She died at the foot of the mountain called Void, and the
clerk at the bridge paid her well, because no one had ever offered up
exactly that.

In her second life she found a wheel in a market town in the third month
and wept without knowing why. She apprenticed. She glazed. She was, the
elders agreed, born to it — and when the Corpse asked again, decades on,
she understood at last what she had been born *for*, and gave it the
wheel again. The second cut was worse, because this time she knew the
shape of the hole.

In her third life she did not seek the wheel and it did not seek her,
and this frightened her more than demons had. She took up the craft
deliberately, in her fortieth year, with the grim tenderness of a woman
visiting a grave she owns. She fired one perfect vessel — Heaven-grade,
the kilnmaster said, weeping — and she carried it up the mountain
unglazed inside, and she gave the Corpse the wheel, the kiln, the
vessel, and the weeping, all of it, the third and final time.

The heavens rang a bell they keep for almost nothing.

In her fourth life she was born in a river town where every house held a
wheel, and she walked past all of them, and felt — nothing. Not the old
ache. Not the pull. Where the wheel had been in her there was now a vast
smooth strength with no handle on it, like a jar fired shut, and her qi
moved through that sealed place *faster*, the way a river runs faster
where the bed is stone. Children asked the strange calm girl why she
never touched clay. "I finished," she said, and could not explain it
better for another two hundred years.

What she never told anyone: sometimes, in deep meditation, she can feel
the vessel. The one that went up the mountain inside her, three lives
ago. It is still there. It is still perfect. It holds everything she
pours into it now — every technique, every insight, every year — and it
never cracks, because she is no longer the kind of thing that grasps its
own making.

The wheel turns without her. That was the point. That was always going
to be the point.
